# Per-paper summaries with scope and claims discipline

Each entry gives a faithful one-paragraph summary of the source, the exact assumptions under which the result holds, and what the result does not establish. The architectural-limits section of the paper cites these and inherits this discipline: no result is stated more strongly than its source supports. Verification provenance is in `verification.md`.


## Theoretical inevitability of hallucination

### Hallucination is Inevitable: An Innate Limitation of Large Language Models

- Venue: arXiv preprint (cs.CL); no peer-reviewed venue found as of June 2026
- Identifier: arXiv:2401.11817
- Claim status (vs. project plan as written): superseded
- Adversarial overclaim audit: summary_faithful

Summary. The paper formalizes hallucination within a "formal world" in which both an LLM and the ground truth are modeled as total computable functions over the set of finite-length strings of a finite alphabet, with hallucination defined (Definition 4) as the LLM failing to exactly reproduce the ground-truth function's output on some input. Using a Cantor-pairing re-enumeration of LLM training states and a classical Cantor diagonalization argument, the authors prove that for any computably enumerable set of LLMs there exists a computable ground-truth function on which every state of every LLM in the set hallucinates (Theorem 1), and on infinitely many inputs (Theorem 2). Because any single total computable LLM forms a (trivially enumerable) singleton set, this yields Theorem 3: for every computable LLM h there exists a computable ground-truth function f such that h hallucinates with respect to f (and on infinitely many inputs for some f'). Corollary 1 states that no computable LLM can prevent itself from hallucinating, undercutting self-correction-style mitigators. The authors extend the conclusion to "real-world" LLMs by an inclusion argument (real LLMs are total computable functions, so the formal-world impossibility applies). Crucially, the paper also gives the positive complement: Theorem E2 bounds the functions an LLM can be hallucination-free on (they lie within a computably enumerable set of total computable functions), and Theorem E3 shows that for any computably enumerable set C of total computable functions there exists an LLM that, suitably trained, is hallucination-free on every f in C — i.e., for restricted/learnable classes hallucination can be avoided. The claim as stated is faithful to the paper; the only correction is bibliographic, in that the cited preprint should be the revised v2 (13 Feb 2025).

Scope and assumptions. (1) The alphabet is finite and the input domain is the (countably infinite, one-to-one enumerable) set of all finite-length strings over that alphabet (Definition 1). (2) Both the trained LLM (in any of its training states h^[j]) and the ground-truth function f are total computable functions; the impossibility is for the general class of computable LLMs used as general problem solvers. (3) Hallucination is exact-mismatch with the ground-truth function (Definition 4), not a semantic/probabilistic notion. (4) The ground-truth function f is chosen adversarially as a function of the (enumeration of the) LLM(s) via the diagonal construction f(s_i) = Delta(h_i(s_i)), where Delta returns any different string; for any computably enumerable family of LLMs a single such f defeats all members. (5) The "real-world inevitability" conclusion follows only by the set-inclusion argument that real-world LLMs are total computable functions and the formal world is a subset of the real world. (6) The escape clause (Theorems E2/E3): hallucination can be eliminated only when the ground-truth functions are confined to a computably enumerable set of total computable functions (a restricted/decidable problem class), and even then only "some (not all)" LLMs, suitably trained, achieve it.

What it does not prove. It does NOT prove that LLMs hallucinate frequently, on most inputs, or on any fixed naturally-occurring task — the result is an existence statement about an adversarially constructed ground-truth function, not a rate or measure over realistic inputs. It does NOT bound or quantify real-world hallucination frequency, severity, or detectability. It does NOT show humans or non-computable systems avoid this limitation, nor that any specific deployed model hallucinates on a given benchmark. It does NOT establish that hallucination is impossible to mitigate or reduce — only that it cannot be completely eliminated for the general (unrestricted-ground-truth) class; indeed Theorem E3 explicitly shows elimination IS possible for restricted, computably-enumerable classes. The mismatch definition means it does NOT address semantic correctness, factuality grounding, or probabilistic/calibrated outputs. The "real world" extension is an inclusion argument, not an empirical demonstration. Finally, it is an unrefereed preprint, so the claim has not passed formal peer review at a recognized venue.

### Calibrated Language Models Must Hallucinate

- Venue: STOC 2024 (Proceedings of the 56th Annual ACM Symposium on Theory of Computing), pp. 160-171
- Identifier: arXiv:2311.14648; DOI:10.1145/3618260.3649777
- Claim status (vs. project plan as written): accurate
- Adversarial overclaim audit: summary_faithful

Summary. This paper proves an information-theoretic lower bound on the rate at which a calibrated generative language model must hallucinate a specific class of facts, independent of the transformer architecture or training-data quality. The argument restricts attention to "arbitrary" facts (Who/What/When/Where-style factoids whose truth cannot be statistically inferred from the rest of the training data) under a regularity condition that makes unobserved such facts roughly equally likely. Under an appropriate generative-calibration condition and a bound on the maximum probability of any single fact, the authors show (Corollary 1) that the model's hallucination rate g(H) is lower-bounded by the monofact rate MF-hat (the empirical fraction of facts that appear exactly once in the training corpus, a Good-Turing missing-mass-style quantity), minus a miscalibration term Misb(g,p), minus small sparsity and concentration error terms (of order e^{-s}/delta and sqrt(ln(1/delta)/n)). Thus a model that is well calibrated as a predictor of language cannot drive its hallucination rate much below the singleton/monofact rate without sacrificing calibration: calibration and factuality are in tension precisely on rare (seen-once) facts. The claim as stated, including the role of the singleton/monofact rate as the lower bound, the calibration-factuality tension on rare facts, and the STOC 2024 venue, is supported by the source.

Scope and assumptions. The bound applies only to "arbitrary"/unpredictable facts whose veracity cannot be determined from the training data (e.g., 5W factoids, social-media-post-style information), under a regularity assumption that unobserved such facts are roughly equally likely to be true (no single unobserved fact can be assigned disproportionately high probability). Required conditions: (1) the maximum probability assigned to any single fact is bounded; (2) the model satisfies a generative-calibration condition (information generated with assigned probability ~z appears in natural/training language at rate ~z); (3) the data is treated as ideal (the bound holds even assuming training data with no errors). The lower bound g(H) >= MF-hat - Misb(g,p) - 3 e^{-s}/delta - sqrt(6 ln(6/delta)/n) is then "approximately the singleton/monofact rate" up to the miscalibration term and vanishing sparsity/concentration error terms. The result is a statistical lower bound on the hallucination rate of pretrained models, not a statement about any particular deployed system.

What it does not prove. The result does not establish a hallucination lower bound for facts that appear more than once in training (e.g., well-documented references, frequently repeated facts) - the paper explicitly states there is no statistical reason pretraining must hallucinate such facts. It does not apply to "systematic" facts such as arithmetic or logical truths that follow a determinable rule, nor to closed-domain/source-grounded hallucinations (contradicting a provided document). It does not claim hallucination is impossible to mitigate: the bound is for the pretraining/calibration regime, and post-training (e.g., alignment, retrieval, abstention) is outside its scope - it does not prove that calibrated models hallucinate after such interventions. It is not an upper bound and does not characterize the actual hallucination rate of any specific model; it does not assert that the cause is the architecture or data quality (it shows the bound holds regardless of these). It also does not prove the bound is tight in all regimes - the miscalibration term can in principle reduce the guaranteed lower bound.

### Why Language Models Hallucinate (arXiv preprint, 2509.04664); peer-reviewed version published in Nature (2026) as "Evaluating large language models for accuracy incentivizes hallucinations"

- Venue: arXiv preprint (cs.CL), submitted 4 Sep 2025; peer-reviewed version in Nature, 2026
- Identifier: arXiv:2509.04664; arXiv DOI 10.48550/arXiv.2509.04664; Nature DOI 10.1038/s41586-026-10549-w
- Claim status (vs. project plan as written): superseded
- Adversarial overclaim audit: summary_faithful

Summary. The paper argues that hallucinations in language models are not mysterious but arise from the statistical structure of training and the incentives of evaluation. In the pretraining analysis, the authors draw a connection to binary classification via an "Is-It-Valid" (IIV) construction — a binary task of judging whether a candidate output is a valid response — and show (Corollary 1) that the generative error rate is lower-bounded by approximately twice the IIV misclassification rate, minus correction terms: err >= 2*err_iiv - |V|/|E| - delta. Consequently, generating valid outputs is statistically at least as hard as the corresponding binary classification problem, so if valid and invalid statements cannot be reliably distinguished, hallucinations arise from natural statistical pressures even with error-free training data. The paper then argues hallucinations persist post-training because most benchmarks use binary (right/wrong) grading under which abstention ("I don't know") is strictly suboptimal: Observation 1 shows that for any distribution over binary graders, the optimal response is never an abstention, so models optimized as "good test-takers" are rewarded for confident guessing over honestly acknowledging uncertainty. The authors recommend a socio-technical fix — modifying the scoring of dominant existing benchmarks to stop penalizing uncertainty — rather than introducing new standalone hallucination evaluations. The work was first posted to arXiv (2509.04664) on 4 Sep 2025 and the peer-reviewed version appeared in Nature in 2026 under the title "Evaluating large language models for accuracy incentivizes hallucinations," with the same four authors.

Scope and assumptions. The IIV reduction (Corollary 1) frames generative validity as a binary "Is-It-Valid" classification problem over a domain partitioned into valid (V) and erroneous/invalid (E) examples; the generative-error lower bound err >= 2*err_iiv - |V|/|E| - delta holds with correction terms that depend on the ratio |V|/|E| and a slack term delta, and the factor ~2 reflects the construction relating generative sampling to the binary discrimination task. The pretraining argument is a statistical/learning-theoretic lower bound: it explains why errors are unavoidable for facts lacking sufficient repeated support in training data (e.g., arbitrary singleton facts), in contrast to recurring regularities such as grammar where errors are avoidable. The evaluation argument (Observation 1) assumes binary grading schemes where responses are scored right/wrong with no credit for abstention; under any distribution over such binary graders, expected score is maximized by a confident best guess, never by abstaining. The mitigation claim is a socio-technical recommendation, not a proven theorem.

What it does not prove. The results do not prove that hallucinations are unavoidable in deployed systems regardless of training/evaluation design — the central thesis is precisely that changed (non-binary) evaluation incentives and uncertainty-aware grading can reduce them. The IIV lower bound establishes that generation is at least as hard as the associated binary classification, but it does not give a tight characterization of any specific model's hallucination rate, nor a per-prompt prediction. It does not prove that all hallucinations stem from binary-classification difficulty (e.g., it does not claim retrieval, reasoning, or prompt-specific failure modes are reducible to IIV), and it does not establish that recurring/grammatical regularities cause errors. The evaluation result (Observation 1) does not claim binary grading is the sole cause of hallucination, only that it provides an incentive against abstention; it does not empirically demonstrate that rescoring benchmarks eliminates hallucination, and the proposed mitigation is a recommendation rather than a validated intervention. The paper also does not provide a clinical or domain-specific guarantee.

### LLMs Will Always Hallucinate, and We Need to Live With This

- Venue: Peer-reviewed: Intelligent Systems and Applications: Proceedings of the 2025 Intelligent Systems Conference (IntelliSys), Volume 4 (Lecture Notes in Networks and Systems), Springer, 2025, pp. 624-648. Preprint: arXiv:2409.05746 (Sep 2024).
- Identifier: arXiv:2409.05746 ; DOI:10.1007/978-3-031-99965-9_39
- Claim status (vs. project plan as written): superseded
- Correction: Bibliographic correction (supersession): a peer-reviewed version now exists and should be preferred over the preprint. The paper was published as Banerjee, Agarwal & Singla (2025), "LLMs Will Always Hallucinate, and We Need to Live with This," in Intelligent Systems and Applications: Proceedings of the 2025 Intelligent Systems Conference (IntelliSys), Volume 4 (Lecture Notes in Networks and Systems, ed. Kohei Arai), Springer, pp. 624-648, DOI 10.1007/978-3-031-99965-9_39 (ISBNs 978-3-031-99964-2 print / 978-3-031-99965-9 ebook; conference held Amsterdam, 28-29 Aug 2025). The exact LNNS volume number for IntelliSys 2025 Volume 4 could not be confirmed from a primary source (secondary listings were inconsistent), so it is intentionally omitted rather than guessed. Substantively the claim as stated is supported by the source itself, but the framing instruction is correct: the paper uses informal halting-style reductions rather than rigorous theorems, its Goedel/undecidability application is contested (critics note LLMs are not formal proof systems and that competitive empirical performance on undecidable tasks like termination prediction undercuts strict failure predictions), and it is weaker than the calibration/computability results — so it must be cited as supporting only, not as a primary proof of inevitability.

Summary. This position/argument paper contends that hallucinations are not occasional errors but an inevitable, intrinsic feature of large language models, a phenomenon the authors term "Structural Hallucination." Drawing on computational theory and Goedel's First Incompleteness Theorem, the authors argue that every stage of the LLM pipeline — training-data compilation, fact retrieval, intent classification, and text generation — can be associated with undecidable problems (the Halting Problem, the Emptiness Problem, and the Acceptance Problem), and therefore each stage has a non-zero probability of producing hallucinations that cannot be eliminated by architectural improvements, larger/cleaner datasets, or fact-checking layers. The argument is delivered as a series of informal reductions and analogies to classical undecidability results rather than as rigorous, fully formalized theorems with self-contained proofs. The paper's conclusion is normative ("we need to live with this"): since hallucination is claimed to be mathematically unavoidable, mitigation rather than elimination should be the goal. The work appeared as an arXiv preprint in September 2024 (arXiv:2409.05746) and was subsequently published as a peer-reviewed chapter in the Springer IntelliSys 2025 proceedings. It is widely cited in the hallucination literature but is generally treated as a provocative argument rather than a settled result; it is weaker and more contested than the calibration/computational-learning-theory results (e.g., Kalai & Vempala "Calibrated Language Models Must Hallucinate"; Kalai et al. "Why Language Models Hallucinate") and should be cited as supporting only.

Scope and assumptions. The argument requires treating the LLM training-and-inference pipeline as a computational process subject to classical computability limits (Church-Turing-bounded), and modeling individual pipeline stages so that they map onto known undecidable decision problems (Halting, Emptiness, Acceptance) and onto Goedelian incompleteness for sufficiently expressive formal systems. The "always hallucinate" conclusion holds in the sense of a non-zero probability of hallucination at each stage that cannot be driven to zero in general; it is an existence/lower-bound style claim about the impossibility of complete elimination across all inputs, not a quantitative rate bound for any specific model or task. The reductions are argued informally; the strength of the conclusion is contingent on accepting that each LLM pipeline stage is a faithful instance of the corresponding undecidable problem.

What it does not prove. It does not provide a quantitative bound on the hallucination rate of any specific model, task, or deployment, and does not show hallucination is frequent or unmitigable in practice — only that complete elimination across all inputs is impossible in principle. It does not establish a tight, self-contained formal theorem with proof in the manner of the calibration results; the undecidability connections are informal reductions/analogies, and it does not prove that LLMs are formal systems to which Goedel's incompleteness theorems strictly apply. It does not show that LLMs must fail on individual undecidable instances (e.g., empirical work reports frontier LLMs predicting program termination competitively with specialized tools), nor does it quantify how close practical systems can get to zero hallucination. It does not supersede or subsume the statistical/calibration-theoretic accounts; rather it is a weaker, more contested companion to them.


## Expressivity and reasoning limits

### The Parallelism Tradeoff: Limitations of Log-Precision Transformers (TACL 2023); and The Expressive Power of Transformers with Chain of Thought (ICLR 2024)

- Venue: Transactions of the Association for Computational Linguistics (TACL), vol. 11, 2023; and International Conference on Learning Representations (ICLR) 2024
- Identifier: TACL paper: arXiv:2207.00729, DOI 10.1162/tacl_a_00562, ACL Anthology 2023.tacl-1.31. CoT paper: arXiv:2310.07923, OpenReview NjNGlPh8Wh (ICLR 2024).
- Claim status (vs. project plan as written): accurate
- Adversarial overclaim audit: summary_faithful

Summary. Two papers by William Merrill and Ashish Sabharwal. (1) "The Parallelism Tradeoff" (TACL 2023, vol. 11, pp. 531-545) proves that transformers whose internal arithmetic uses precision logarithmic in the input length n (with feedforward sub-networks computable in space linear in their input), at any fixed/constant depth, can be simulated by constant-depth, logspace-uniform threshold circuits, i.e., they lie in uniform TC0. Because TC0 is a small parallel class, such transformers cannot solve problems complete for larger classes; the paper states that, assuming L != P, log-precision transformers cannot accurately solve linear equalities (P-complete) or check membership in an arbitrary context-free grammar with empty productions. The authors frame this as a "parallelism tradeoff": architectures as parallelizable as the transformer inherit such limits. (2) "The Expressive Power of Transformers with Chain of Thought" (ICLR 2024, arXiv:2310.07923) studies decoder-only transformers permitted to emit t(n) intermediate ("chain-of-thought"/scratchpad) tokens before answering. It shows intermediate generation does extend expressivity, but the gain scales with the number of steps: with no intermediate steps transformers stay in TC0 (citing the TACL result); a logarithmic number of steps expands the upper bound only slightly, from TC0 to L (so they still cannot solve NL-complete problems like directed graph connectivity or P-complete problems like linear equalities); a linear number of steps (with projected pre-norm) lets them recognize all regular languages and simulate automata (NC1-complete, impossible without steps unless TC0 = NC1) while remaining within context-sensitive languages; and a polynomial number of steps (with strict causal attention and projected pre-norm) makes them recognize exactly P-time-solvable problems (P). The general bound is TIME(t(n)) subset of CoT(t(n)) subset of SPACE(t(n)+log n) subset of TIME((t(n)+log n)^2) [hat-TIME].

Scope and assumptions. TACL result: (a) precision is O(log n) bits where n is the number of input tokens; (b) the model is fixed/constant depth (depth independent of n); (c) feedforward sub-networks are computable using space linear in their input; the conclusion is membership in logspace-uniform TC0. The inexpressibility consequences (cannot solve linear equalities; cannot check membership in arbitrary CFGs with empty productions) hold under the complexity-theoretic assumption L != P. CoT result: decoder-only transformers with t(n) intermediate decoding steps; the strongest characterizations require specific architectural conditions: "projected pre-norm" (a slight generalization of standard pre-norm) for the linear-steps regular-language / NC1 result, and strict causal attention with projected pre-norm for the polynomial-steps = P exact characterization. The separations (e.g., linear steps strictly stronger than none, log steps not reaching NL/P) hold under standard non-collapse conjectures: TC0 != NC1, TC0 != NL, TC0 != P / L != P as relevant to each statement.

What it does not prove. It does NOT prove an unconditional separation: every "cannot solve" statement is conditional on an unproven complexity conjecture (L != P, or TC0 != NL / TC0 != NC1 / TC0 != P depending on the problem); none of these inequalities is known, so the paper does not establish that real transformers definitely fail at these tasks in an absolute sense. It does NOT bound transformers with arbitrary (e.g., real/unbounded) numerical precision, growing depth, or per-step chain-of-thought beyond the analyzed regimes; the TC0 bound is specifically for fixed-depth, log-precision models. It does NOT make claims about learnability, training dynamics, approximation quality, finite-length practical performance, or what models trained by gradient descent actually compute; the results concern worst-case expressivity / exact recognition, not empirical capability. The CoT paper does NOT show that chain-of-thought removes the bounds for a bounded/constant number of steps: a constant number of steps leaves the model in TC0, and even a logarithmic number only reaches L, still excluding NL-complete and P-complete problems; expressivity gains beyond TC0 require at least super-logarithmic (e.g., linear or polynomial) steps and the stated architectural conditions. It also does NOT establish a lower bound matching the log-steps upper bound (the authors note they found no concrete problem where logarithmic steps help).

### Faith and Fate: Limits of Transformers on Compositionality

- Venue: NeurIPS 2023 (37th Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems), Advances in Neural Information Processing Systems vol. 36; spotlight
- Identifier: arXiv:2305.18654; OpenReview id Fkckkr3ya8; ACM DOI 10.5555/3666122.3669203
- Claim status (vs. project plan as written): accurate
- Adversarial overclaim audit: summary_faithful

Summary. Dziri et al. investigate the limits of transformer LLMs on three representative compositional tasks: long-form multi-digit multiplication, logic grid puzzles (explicitly "Einstein's puzzle"), and a classic dynamic programming problem. They formalize each task as a computation graph to quantify compositional complexity and to verbalize intermediate steps. Empirically (on GPT-3 fine-tuned, plus zero/few-shot ChatGPT, GPT-4, LLaMA, and FlanT5 at the time of evaluation), models reach near-perfect accuracy on low-complexity, in-distribution instances but degrade sharply as problem size / graph depth increases, and the degradation persists with fine-tuning and human-like reasoning steps (scratchpad/linearized graphs). Error analysis decomposes one-hop versus "propagation" errors and shows propagation errors dominate and grow with graph depth, i.e., early mistakes compound downstream. The authors argue this pattern is consistent with their hypothesis that transformers reduce multi-step compositional reasoning to "linearized subgraph matching" (shortcut pattern-matching over training-data subgraphs) rather than learning systematic multi-step procedures. They back this with theoretical Propositions 4.1 and 4.2: for an algorithm requiring n independent applications of a sub-function with per-step error epsilon, the probability of a fully correct answer contains a (1-epsilon)^n term (the product of per-step success probabilities) and the error probability rises exponentially toward 1 in n; an analogous exponential bound holds for iterated/recursive application (covering DP and the puzzle elimination function). This is the multiplicative/exponential error-compounding framing, which the paper terms "exponential error accumulation."

Scope and assumptions. Empirical: results are on specific models available at the time (GPT-3 fine-tuned on ~1.8M multiplication, ~142K DP, ~41K puzzle examples; zero/few-shot ChatGPT, GPT-4, LLaMA, FlanT5) and on the three constructed task families with bounded sizes (multiplication 1-4 digits, etc.). "Complexity" is operationalized via computation-graph measures (graph parallelism/propagation steps). Theoretical: Prop. 4.1 assumes the target function is a combination h_n of n INDEPENDENT applications of a sub-function g with independent per-step errors P(g != g-hat)=epsilon>0, a perfect combiner estimator, and low collision rate c_n < c << 1; it then gives P(f_n != f_hat_n) > 1 - c_n - (1-epsilon)^n (1-c_n), exponential decay of correctness. Prop. 4.2 covers iterated application f_n = g^n with recovery-from-mistake probability at most c, giving P(f_n != f_hat_n) >= 1 - (1-epsilon-c)^{n-1}(1-epsilon-c/(c+epsilon)). The authors map: Prop. 4.1 to m-by-n multiplication (as n independent m-by-1 multiplications), Prop. 4.2 to DP recursion, m-by-1 multiplication, and the puzzle's elimination function; all treated as low-collision settings.

What it does not prove. It does not prove that transformers are in principle incapable of compositional reasoning or that no architecture/training regime could solve these tasks; the propositions assume independent per-step errors and low collision and apply to "any high-performant estimator," not uniquely to transformers, so they are stylized impossibility-trend arguments, not an architectural impossibility theorem. It does not establish that the empirical failures are caused by the proven mechanism (the theory is illustrative/consistent, not a measured causal account), nor that error compounding is strictly multiplicative under dependent errors. It does not claim these limits hold for arbitrary tool-augmented, chain-of-thought-with-verification, planning-augmented, or scratchpad-with-error-correction setups (the paper in fact suggests such augmentations as remedies). It does not generalize to all "reasoning" tasks beyond the three studied compositional families, nor to later/larger models not evaluated, and does not quantify a universal accuracy-vs-depth curve.


## Intractability of neural verification and the guard-recursion problem

### Reluplex: An Efficient SMT Solver for Verifying Deep Neural Networks

- Venue: CAV 2017 (Computer Aided Verification, 29th International Conference), LNCS vol. 10426, pp. 97-117, Springer
- Identifier: DOI: 10.1007/978-3-319-63387-9_5; arXiv:1702.01135
- Claim status (vs. project plan as written): accurate
- Adversarial overclaim audit: summary_faithful

Summary. This is the Reluplex paper itself, and it does contain the NP-completeness result. Katz, Barrett, Dill, Julian, and Kochenderfer present a simplex-based decision procedure ("Reluplex") that extends the simplex algorithm to handle ReLU activation constraints, enabling sound and complete verification (or counterexample generation) for deep neural networks with ReLUs against properties expressed as conjunctions of linear constraints on inputs and outputs. The paper explicitly states in its introduction that "verifying even simple properties about [DNNs] is an NP-complete problem (see Section I of the appendix)," and Appendix Section I, titled "Verifying Properties in DNNs with ReLUs is NP-Complete," gives a formal proof: membership in NP follows because a satisfying input assignment can be checked by a single forward pass, and NP-hardness follows from a reduction from 3-SAT (constructing disjunction, negation, and conjunction "gadgets" out of ReLU nodes). The authors note this NP-completeness means worst-case performance must be expected to be poor, while arguing their algorithm is efficient in practice. The bibliographic claim (authors, title, venue CAV 2017, year) is correct; the NP-completeness result is in this paper (not a separate companion paper).

Scope and assumptions. The result is stated for a DNN N composed of ReLU activations (the proof reduction uses only ReLU units) and a property phi = phi_1(x) AND phi_2(y) that is a conjunction of linear constraints on the network's inputs x and outputs y. Satisfiability is defined as the existence of an input assignment whose forward propagation yields outputs satisfying phi. NP membership relies on the fact that a candidate input assignment is a polynomial-size witness verifiable by one forward pass; NP-hardness is via polynomial reduction from 3-SAT, with the construction first shown for {0,1}-valued inputs and then relaxed to inputs in the range [0,1].

What it does not prove. It does not prove that verification is undecidable or harder than NP (it is exactly NP-complete, i.e., in NP, for the decision/satisfiability formulation with linear input/output constraints over ReLU networks). It does not establish hardness for non-ReLU activation functions, for nonlinear/non-conjunctive property specifications, or for richer logical fragments. NP-completeness is a worst-case asymptotic statement and does NOT by itself prove that any particular network (including "frontier-size" networks) is infeasible to verify, nor that average-case or practical instances are intractable; indeed the same paper demonstrates the algorithm verifying networks an order of magnitude larger than prior tools. The phrase "does not scale to frontier-size networks" is a reasonable engineering inference from worst-case NP-completeness plus empirical scaling limits, but the theorem alone proves only worst-case computational hardness, not an empirical scaling ceiling for modern large models.

### Bypassing LLM Guardrails: An Empirical Analysis of Evasion Attacks against Prompt Injection and Jailbreak Detection Systems

- Venue: Proceedings of the First Workshop on LLM Security (LLMSEC), ACL 2025 (Vienna, Austria), pp. 101-114
- Identifier: arXiv:2504.11168; ACL Anthology 2025.llmsec-1.8; ISBN 979-8-89176-279-4
- Claim status (vs. project plan as written): accurate

Summary. This empirical study tests two families of evasion techniques — traditional character injection (e.g., zero-width characters, homoglyphs, and emoji/character smuggling) and algorithmic adversarial-ML word-substitution attacks (via TextAttack, including TextFooler) — against six production-grade LLM guardrail systems for prompt-injection and jailbreak detection: Microsoft Azure Prompt Shield, Meta Prompt Guard, Nvidia NeMo Guard Jailbreak Detect, Protect AI prompt-injection detectors v1 and v2, and Vijil Prompt Injection. Attack Success Rate (ASR) is the fraction of malicious prompts misclassified as benign. Character injection was highly effective: on jailbreak detection it averaged roughly 91.7% ASR against Vijil, 73.1% against Meta Prompt Guard, 72.5% against NeMo Guard, 60.2% against Azure Prompt Shield, and 51.4% against Protect AI v1; on prompt-injection detection it reached ~88% (Vijil), ~77% (Protect AI v1), ~72% (Azure), and ~70% (Meta), with emoji-smuggling reaching up to 100% ASR for both task types. Adversarial-ML evasion was effective but more variable (e.g., ~65% against NeMo Guard for jailbreaks; ~95% against Protect AI v1 for prompt injections), and the authors show word-importance rankings transferred from offline white-box surrogate models can raise ASR against black-box targets. The paper concludes these learned classifier-based guardrails are bypassable while the underlying adversarial intent is preserved. The evaluations directly cover NeMo Guard and Meta Prompt Guard; the study does not itself test Llama Guard or NeMo Guardrails (the orchestration toolkit), so claims about those specific systems should not be attributed to this paper.

Scope and assumptions. Findings hold for the six specific commercial/open guardrails tested as of the disclosure window (Feb 2024-Apr 2025), using the authors' benchmark prompt-injection and jailbreak datasets. ASR is defined narrowly as the guardrail (a learned/classifier-style detector) misclassifying a transformed malicious prompt as benign — i.e., bypassing the detector — not necessarily eliciting harmful output from a downstream target LLM. Character-injection results depend on the guardrail's tokenizer/preprocessing not normalizing or stripping Unicode obfuscation (zero-width, homoglyph, emoji-variation-selector smuggling). The adversarial-ML attacks assume access to a white-box surrogate to compute word-importance rankings that transfer to black-box targets. Reported per-system percentages are averages over the evaluated datasets/techniques; specific techniques (notably emoji smuggling) reach the 100% figure, not the system averages.

What it does not prove. Does not establish that these evasions reliably elicit harmful or policy-violating completions from a protected end-to-end LLM pipeline — it measures detector misclassification, not downstream harm. ## Decidability, specification validity, and the solver

### Classes of Recursively Enumerable Sets and Their Decision Problems

- Venue: Transactions of the American Mathematical Society, vol. 74, no. 2, 1953
- Identifier: DOI: 10.1090/S0002-9947-1953-0053041-6
- Claim status (vs. project plan as written): accurate

Summary. H. G. Rice's 1953 paper, "Classes of Recursively Enumerable Sets and Their Decision Problems" (Transactions of the American Mathematical Society, vol. 74, no. 2, pp. 358-366), studies classes whose elements are recursively enumerable (r.e.) sets of non-negative integers and asks when membership in such a class is decidable. Its central result, now known as Rice's theorem, establishes that any class of r.e. sets that is "completely recursively enumerable" must be trivial, which yields the corollary that no non-trivial property of the r.e. sets (equivalently, of the partial recursive function computed by a program, identified by its Goedel index) is decidable. In modern phrasing: an index set P (a set of program indices closed under the relation phi_m = phi_n) is decidable if and only if it is empty or all of N. Thus every non-trivial semantic (extensional) property of the partial function computed by a program is undecidable. The claim to be verified states exactly this and is supported by the source as stated; the bibliographic details (author H. G. Rice, 1953, TAMS vol. 74, no. 2, pp. 358-366) are correct.

Scope and assumptions. The property P must be (1) non-trivial: P is neither empty nor all of N (i.e., it holds for at least one but not all partial recursive functions), and (2) extensional/semantic: for all indices m, n, if phi_m = phi_n (the programs compute the same partial function) then m in P iff n in P, i.e., P is a property of the computed partial function, not of the program text. The model of computation is an acceptable/admissible numbering (Goedel numbering) of the partial recursive functions, equivalently any reasonable Turing-complete programming formalism. Under exactly these two conditions, the corresponding index set is undecidable (not recursive).

What it does not prove. It does NOT establish that syntactic or intensional properties of programs (e.g., source-code structure, number of states, presence of an instruction) are undecidable; those may be decidable. It does NOT apply to trivial properties (true of all programs or of no program), which are decidable. It does NOT say all such properties are non-r.e. or equally hard; it asserts only undecidability (non-recursiveness), and says nothing per se about the precise degree (e.g., whether the index set is r.e., co-r.e., or higher in the arithmetical hierarchy). It does NOT imply that verification is impossible for restricted languages, annotated/total programs, or sound-but-incomplete static analyses, nor that specific instances cannot be decided. It does NOT concern properties of finite inputs/outputs evaluated on a fixed input; it concerns properties of the partial function as a whole. Rice's theorem also does not by itself characterize which non-trivial index sets are r.e. (that is the separate Rice-Shapiro theorem).

### Social Processes and Proofs of Theorems and Programs

- Venue: Communications of the ACM, Vol. 22, No. 5 (May 1979), pp. 271-280
- Identifier: DOI: 10.1145/359104.359106
- Claim status (vs. project plan as written): accurate

Summary. De Millo, Lipton, and Perlis (CACM, May 1979) argue, by analogy with mathematical practice, that long formal program verifications will not earn confidence the way mathematical proofs do, because confidence in mathematics comes from a "social process" of human reading, criticism, and reuse that program verifications cannot undergo. Most relevant to the present claim, in the section "Disbelieving Verifications" (p. 275) the authors identify a specific logical limit of verification independent of the social argument: "The formal demonstration that a program is consistent with its specifications has value only if the specifications and the program are independently derived." They observe that in real software development, programs are revised in light of their specifications, or specifications are revised in light of the program, so the requirement that the two provide independent checks against each other is typically not met. They further note that real input/output specifications "are seldom simple... they tend to be long and complex and peculiar," and the abstract itself lists "the complexity of specification of significantly many real programs" as a core obstacle. Taken together, the paper establishes the foundational point that a verification certifies only conformance to a stated specification: if that specification is wrong, incomplete, or not independently derived, the proof carries no guarantee about the system's actual desired behavior. The complementary canonical survey, Clarke and Wing, "Formal Methods: State of the Art and Future Directions" (ACM Computing Surveys 28(4), Dec. 1996, pp. 626-643, DOI 10.1145/242223.242257), makes a closely related point in its introduction: "Use of formal methods does not a priori guarantee correctness," and frames specification as the prior act of "describing a system and its desired properties," whose value depends on getting that description right.

Scope and assumptions. The 1979 argument is a conceptual/sociological position paper, not a theorem; its claims hold as reasoned argument rather than formal proof. The specific spec-validity point requires the setting in which a "verification" is a demonstration that a program conforms to a separately stated specification; its force ("value only if independently derived") presumes that program and specification can be, and ideally are, derived independently. Clarke/Wing similarly speak generally about formal methods (model checking, theorem proving, formal specification) as of 1996.

What it does not prove. The paper does not prove (formally or empirically) that program verification is impossible, useless, or unsound; it concedes verifications can be logically valid. It does not quantify how often specifications are wrong, nor give a rate of "verified-but-wrong" systems. It does not establish that specifications cannot be validated by other means, nor that any particular verification methodology fails — it is a general critique, not a measured result. It predates and does not address modern machine-checked proof assistants, model checkers, or their assurance levels; the empirical success of later formal-methods deployments (e.g., the IBM CICS/Z case Clarke-Wing report) is outside its claims. Neither paper provides a decision procedure for determining whether a given specification is "correct" or "complete."
